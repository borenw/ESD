First-principles teardown

Every ESD kill is either
over-voltage or over-current.

An ESD event dumps a fixed packet of charge into a node. Silicon dies in exactly two ways depending on how that charge is spent: the transient field ruptures a dielectric before current can flow, or the transient current heats a junction or conductor until it melts. Fast events favor the first channel, slow/high-energy events the second. Everything below is organized around that split.

Over-voltage / field-driven channel Over-current / thermal channel Both (event triggers one, finishes via the other)

01 — Root cause

The two root causes

Strip the acronyms away and there are only two physical destruction channels. They differ in what is exceeded (a critical field vs. a melting temperature) and in which timescale favors them. A well-designed protection network has to win on both: clamp the voltage fast enough and conduct the current with enough robustness.

Channel A — field driven

OVER-VOLTAGE

The transient voltage across a thin dielectric or a reverse junction exceeds its breakdown field before meaningful current flows. The dielectric ruptures; a conductive filament forms.

E = V / d  >  E_crit (≈ 6–10 MV/cm for SiO₂)
  • Wins on fast edges — CDM, IEC first peak — where the clamp cannot turn on in time and voltage overshoots.
  • Damage is local to the thinnest oxide / sharpest junction nearest the pad.
  • Signature: gate leakage, low-ohm gate-to-drain/source short.
Channel B — thermal driven

OVER-CURRENT

Current is conducted, but I²R heating in a junction, contact, via, or resistor drives local temperature past the melt point. Resistance collapses and a runaway filament shorts the structure.

P = I²R → T > T_melt (Si 1414 °C, Al 660 °C)
  • Wins on high-energy / longer events — HBM, IEC second peak, MM.
  • Quantified per device by the TLP second-breakdown current It2.
  • Signature: junction leakage, fused metal/contact, shifted resistor value.
They chain. Over-voltage often initiates and over-current finishes: a field punctures the oxide → a low-ohm path appears → current crowds into it → Joule heating melts a filament. When you debug, decide which channel started the event, because that's the one your design has to defeat.
02 — Stress models

Stress models & delivered current

Each qualification model is just a defined RC source delivering a known charge packet. The headline number is peak current, which sets how hard the over-current channel is pushed, and the rise time, which sets whether the over-voltage channel even gets a chance (a clamp that is fast for HBM can be too slow for CDM/IEC).

Levels differ between models, so this compares relative severity at the level each part is actually qualified to — which is the real design message: a part that survives 2 kV HBM can still die at 2 kV IEC, because IEC delivers several times the current with a sub-nanosecond edge. MM (200 pF, near-zero series R) is largely deprecated by JEDEC but still appears in legacy specs.

04 — Thermal channel

Over-current mechanisms I

Here the current is being conducted — the question is whether the path can survive the heat. Mitigation is about It2, ballasting, conductor cross-section, and a low-impedance discharge route.

MechanismPhysical causeWhereGoverning relationElectrical signatureWorst model
2nd breakdown / filamentation I²R heat melts silicon; molten Si resistance drops ~30×, current crowds into the hot spot → thermal runaway filament drain-to-source. Drain junction near surface, gate-drain overlap of the I/O / clamp FET. I > It2 (TLP) Low-ohm S-D short; junction leakage. HBM
Metal / contact / via fuse Surge through an undersized conductor or via array fuses it open; Al can spike into Si at contacts. Narrow ESD bus, single-via necks, pad routing. P_fail ∝ t−1/2 (Wunsch–Bell) Open circuit, or intermittent high-R. HBM/MM

Always run the diff. Stress a pin you know shares the same clamp and passes, against the failing pin. If the reference passes at the same level, the clamp is fine and your problem is the path to it (series R, floating-well switch, narrow bus) — not the clamp device.
07 — Your case

Mapping to the SV* pins

From the pin/ESD diagram, the SVID signals sit in two domains, and the high-voltage outputs SVDH / SVTOH are exposed to both damage channels. That is why a single test rarely tells the whole story here.

Over-voltage risk

SVDH · SVTOH gate oxide of HDrive PMOS

On a fast edge (CDM, IEC first peak) the main prgVddioCDG clamp is far across the VDDIO net and may turn on too late. The HDrive PMOS gate/oxide nearest the pad sees the overshoot → oxide rupture. Signature would be gate leakage / low-ohm gate short with the clamp intact.

Confirm: vf-TLP / CDM; EMMI at the HDrive gate; measure L·di/dt + R from pad to clamp.

Over-current risk

SVDH · SVTOH discharge path to clamp

On HBM, current must reach prgVddioCDG. If a prgVddioSwitch 1 kΩ leg or the prgGpioR floating-well switch sits in series, the path is strangled and current crowds into the HDrive drain junction → 2nd breakdown. Signature: low-ohm drain short, low It2.

Confirm: TLP It2 on SVDH→PGND / →VDDIO both polarities; diff vs. a clean VDDIO pin; OBIRCH the filament.

Net takeaway for SV*: qualify these pins on TLP/HBM and vf-TLP/CDM. A dedicated dual-diode cell at the pad that bypasses the programmable 1 kΩ / floating-well isolation defeats both channels at once — fast local clamp for the field, low-impedance route for the current.
