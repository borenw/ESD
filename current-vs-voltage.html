1. Guide — is CDM vs HBM a current/voltage test, or a duration trade-off?

Short version: it's a useful first guess, but the clean variable is duration, not the model name. The model names just sit at opposite ends of the pulse-width axis, and pulse width is what decides whether heat or field reaches its limit first.

1.1 The heuristic, and why it's only a heuristic

The common shorthand — HBM = current damage, CDM = voltage damage — holds often enough to be useful: HBM failures are usually thermal (second breakdown, metal/contact fusing), and CDM failures are usually dielectric (gate-oxide rupture)[6]. But it is a correlation, not a definition, and it breaks in two ways:

  • A CDM strike can still cause thermal damage, and an HBM strike can rupture an oxide — the model only shifts the probability of each channel.
  • Pass/fail on CDM vs HBM also confounds polarity, the discharge path, and which internal node sees the stress. It tells you a pin failed, not which physical limit it hit.
i
Cleaner experimentTo actually separate current from voltage, sweep pulse width directly: TLP (≈100 ns) gives you the thermal second-breakdown current It2; vf-TLP (≈1–5 ns) exposes transient turn-on and overshoot. The pulse-width sweep is the discriminator; CDM and HBM are just two points on it[3].

1.2 The real axis: pulse width (Wunsch–Bell)

Thermal failure is governed by how much power a structure can absorb before a junction or conductor reaches its melt point, and that limit is a strong function of pulse width. The Wunsch–Bell relation[1] gives three regimes of power-to-failure Pf versus pulse width τ:

  • Adiabatic (very short): Pf ∝ 1/τ — no time for heat to spread, so it takes enormous power to fail thermally.
  • Thermal-diffusion (mid): Pf ∝ 1/√τ — the classic Wunsch–Bell slope.
  • Steady-state (long / EOS): Pf ≈ const.

The consequence is the key to your question: as pulses get shorter, the thermal limit climbs steeply, but the dielectric (field) limit barely moves with time — an oxide ruptures at roughly its critical field whether the pulse is 1 ns or 100 ns[2]. The device fails at whichever limit is lower. So there is a crossover: long pulses fail thermally (current), short pulses fail dielectrically (voltage).

1.3 So what does "sustained longer" damage?

Longer events deliver more ∫ I²R dt into the same structure, so the answer is unambiguous: longer duration favors the thermal / over-current channel.

Damage that a longer-duration event preferentially causes
MechanismWhy duration mattersChannel
Junction 2nd breakdown / filamentationHeat must accumulate until Si melts and resistance collapses (~30×) into a runaway filament; needs time at current.I
Metal / contact / via fusingWunsch–Bell t−1/2 regime — the longer the pulse, the lower the current that fuses the conductor.I
Ballast / sense resistor burnoutIntegrated power raises the resistor past its melt/shift point.I
EOS (ms+)Steady-state thermal regime — pure heat; not ESD anymore but the same channel taken to its limit.I

The flip side: the shortest events (CDM, IEC first peak) don't have time to deposit that heat, so they fail things by field instead — oxide rupture, junction overshoot — which is exactly why the HBM↔TLP thermal correlation breaks down once a non-thermal oxide mechanism enters[3].

1.4 With a voltage clamp present: CDM AC-couples past it

This is the part your note got right. A clamp limits the pad voltage, but it does so with finite turn-on delay and finite impedance, and CDM's sub-nanosecond edge has frequency content up into the GHz. Three things let CDM bite even behind a working clamp:

  1. Turn-on delay. An RC-triggered clamp that turns on in a few ns is fast for HBM but can lag a <0.4 ns CDM edge — the node overshoots before the big FET conducts[9].
  2. L·di/dt overshoot. Even while conducting, the residual voltage is Vhold + I·Ron + L·di/dt. CDM's huge di/dt across bond/bus inductance can push this above the gate-oxide breakdown voltage — a documented CDM failure mode[4].
  3. Capacitive (AC) coupling. i = C·dV/dt: a fast edge drives displacement current through Cgd / Miller / well caps straight into internal gates. The clamp pins the DC level on the rail; the high-frequency transient still arrives at the oxide the clamp never sees.
✦
Net answer to 1.1Yes — once a clamp is in the loop, CDM is even more a voltage/field problem than a current problem. The clamp absorbs the bulk charge (the current channel), so what's left to do damage is the fast residual that couples and overshoots into oxide — field channel. HBM, by contrast, parks the clamp in steady conduction, so its risk is the current the clamp and bus must survive.

2. Root cause with the attached clamp — AC trigger + snapback

The clamp cells repeated across your pin map (IS_AVNWELL, Pvdd2AnaVddio, PVDD2POC, prgVddioCDG) are all the same structure. Reading it correctly is what lets you assign a failure to the current or the voltage channel.

2.1 What the clamp in the diagram actually is

The resistor + capacitor, inverter, and large output FET with a parallel diode is a textbook RC-triggered active MOSFET rail clamp[5], usually paired with pad steering diodes (one up to the rail, one up from ground)[4]. It is a transient-triggered clamp: it responds to the rising edge of an ESD event, not to a fixed voltage.

2.2 Two mechanisms: AC trigger and snapback backstop

Your read — "combo of AC + snapback" — is exactly the operating principle. They are two different ways the same cell can turn on:

The clamp's two turn-on mechanisms
MechanismTriggerSpeed / behaviorRisk
AC trigger primary Displacement current in the RC network (responds to dV/dt) — not to a fixed voltage. Turns MBIG on in a few ns; stays on for the RC time-out (hundreds of ns), clamping the rail in triode at low voltage[10]. If the RC/gate drive is slower than the edge (CDM), it lags → overshoot before conduction.
Snapback backstop Avalanche at the drain junction fires the parasitic lateral NPN (current-triggered). Higher trigger Vt1, then a low holding voltage; carries large current once on. Non-uniform conduction → filamentation if un-ballasted; Vt1 can exceed oxide BV in advanced nodes[2].

2.3 Two phases = two damage channels

The single most useful way to assign root cause: a clamp event has a turn-on/overshoot phase and a conduction phase, and each phase owns one damage channel.

Phase → channel → where the damage lands
PhaseWhat's happeningChannel & failureDriven by
Turn-on / overshootBefore MBIG (or snapback) fully conducts, the node rises to Vt1 + L·di/dt; fast edge AC-couples to internal gates.Over-voltage — oxide rupture, junction overshoot.CDM, IEC first peak (fast edges).
ConductionMBIG/snapback ON, sinking the discharge; residual V = Vhold + I·Ron + L·di/dt.Over-current — 2nd breakdown, metal/contact fuse.HBM, MM, IEC second peak (long tails).

2.4 Diagnosis & fixes by phase

i
If it fails fast (CDM / vf-TLP) but survives HBMYou're in the turn-on/overshoot phase → voltage channel. Look at trigger speed and overshoot: shrink the effective RC for the edge or use a capacitance multiplier / drain-gate coupling to speed turn-on[9], lower Vt1, add a local secondary clamp at the receiver, a small series gate resistor, and dual CDM diodes placed right at the pad.
!
If it fails long (HBM / 100 ns TLP)You're in the conduction phase → current channel. Look at It2 and the discharge path: enlarge MBIG, add ballasting/silicide-block, widen the bus and via arrays, and — critically for your SV* case — get the series 1 kΩ (prgVddioSwitch) and the floatwell switch out of the ESD path so current actually reaches prgVddioCDG.
✓
SV* takeawayBecause SVDH / SVTOH sit behind a far clamp and programmable isolation, they're exposed in both phases. Qualify on vf-TLP/CDM (overshoot) and TLP/HBM (It2), and a pad-local dual-diode + secondary clamp that bypasses the 1 kΩ/floatwell defeats both phases at once.
