Chapter 1 · where we started

1. The SV* ESD problem

The pin map shows the SVID signals split across two ESD domains, and the high-voltage outputs sit behind programmable isolation that can strand the discharge.

  • Two-domain SVID. Low-voltage I/O — SVC, SVDL, SVTI, SVTOL — lives in the Pvdd2AnaVddio / prgCustomIo3 group, clamped to PGND. The high-voltage outputs — SVDH, SVTOH — sit in the far-right prgSvidio2VddioHDrive block referenced to VDDIO.
  • The yellow failure path. A positive strike on SVDH must travel up into the VDDIO net, across to the prgVddioCDG clamp, then down to PGND.
  • The stranded path. Two programmable elements poison that route for ESD: the prgVddioSwitch 1 kΩ legs (≈2 kV drop at HBM peak when the bridge FET is off) and the prgGpioR floatwell switch (debiases under a fast transient). If current can't reach the clamp, it crowds into the HDrive PMOS drain junction.
Core suspicion

SVDH/SVTOH are exposed in both channels: a fast edge overshoots the far clamp onto the HDrive gate oxide (voltage), and a long event can't reach the clamp through the 1 kΩ/floatwell so it melts the drain (current). The rest of this document is the framework to prove which.

3.1 The Wunsch–Bell crossover

Thermal power-to-failure climbs steeply as pulses shorten (∝1/√τ in the diffusion regime), while the dielectric limit barely moves with time. The device fails at whichever limit is lower — so there is a crossover.[1]

So "longer duration" damages by current. More ∫I²R dt into the same spot → junction melt, metal/contact fuse, resistor burnout — all thermal.

Chapter 4 · the protection device

4. The clamp — AC trigger + snapback

The cells repeated across the pin map (IS_AVNWELL, PVDD2POC, prgVddioCDG…) are all the same: an RC-triggered active MOSFET rail clamp.[5] It turns on two ways.

The clamp's two turn-on mechanisms
MechanismTriggerBehaviourRisk
AC trigger primaryDisplacement current in the RC net — responds to dV/dt, not a fixed voltageTurns the big FET on in ~ns; holds for the RC time-out (hundreds of ns), clamping in triode at low VIf RC/gate drive is slower than the edge (CDM) → overshoot before conduction
Snapback backstopDrain-junction avalanche fires the parasitic NPN (current-triggered)Higher Vt1, then low holding voltage; carries large currentNon-uniform → filamentation if un-ballasted; Vt1 can exceed oxide BV in scaled nodes

4.1 Two phases = two channels

A clamp event has a turn-on/overshoot phase and a conduction phase, and each owns one damage channel.

5. CDM vs HBM — diagnosis by duration

Three physical claims from the session, each checked against published sources. All three hold.

Physics claims — verdicts
ClaimVerdictWhy (sourced)
HBM (longer) → more current/thermal damage; energy ∫I²R dt accumulates, melts metal/junctionCorrectWunsch–Bell: in the diffusion regime power-to-fail falls as 1/√τ, so longer pulses melt at lower current; HBM (130–170 ns) fails by junction melt & metal fuse.[1][3]
CDM (short, <1 ns) → voltage stress; edge outruns the RC clampCorrectCDM rise <200 ps; documented oxide damage from the ~20 ps initial step before the clamp conducts; oxide rupture is the dominant CDM failure mode.[8][9]

Chapter 7 · bench workflow

7. The diagnostic flow

Each step measures something that tells you what to measure next.

Pre-stress curve trace (baseline)

DC leakage on the suspect pin and its neighbours, recorded in the same grounding you'll zap in (Ch 8–9). This is your "before" signature.

▼

Read the failure signature

Gate leakage / low-ohm gate short → voltage. Drain–source short / junction leakage → current. Open on a bus → metal fuse.

▼

TLP (100 ns) — measure It2

If current is suspected: sweep to failure, capture Vt1, Vhold, It2, Rdyn. That's the thermal limit in conduction.

▼

vf-TLP (1–5 ns) — measure overshoot

If voltage is suspected: does the node overshoot oxide BV before the clamp turns on? Did snapback trigger?

▼

Pulse-width sweep (1 / 10 / 30 / 100 / 300 ns)

The decisive test. If It2 falls steeply with width → thermal/current. If failure stays oxide regardless of width → field/voltage. Builds your own Wunsch–Bell curve.

▼

CDM vs HBM on an identical pair (validate)

One part CDM, one part HBM. Different signatures → duration is the variable. Same signature → the path/clamp is the limit.

▼

Failure analysis + fix

EMMI / OBIRCH / FIB to pinpoint the filament, then fix the owning phase (faster clamp for voltage, lower-R path / bigger FET for current) and re-qualify.

7.1 How OBIRCH builds its map

OBIRCH (Optical Beam Induced Resistance Change) localizes resistive defects one pixel at a time. A constant voltage holds the net biased; an IR laser (≈1.3 µm, so it works through the silicon backside) is raster-scanned, and at each beam position the laser locally warms the metal. Metal resistance rises with temperature, so under fixed V the supply current shifts by ΔI/I = −ΔR/R. The tool samples that supply current at every pixel and converts it to a resistance-change image.[15] So yes — the map is the supply current measured under constant voltage.

Your model, corrected

Right: constant V, measure supply current, defect = outlier. Refine: it's one raster scan with the no-laser baseline rejected continuously by laser-modulation + lock-in — not two full laser-off / laser-on images subtracted (that's golden-device differencing, a separate method). And the contrast is an anomalous resistance change — high local R, current crowding, or a void that impedes heat spread — not "trapped heat" alone.[15][16]

Concretely, picture the classic V — ammeter — R loop. The chip net is the resistor; constant V holds it biased; the ammeter is the detector. The laser sets where on the die you are (the pixel's x,y); the ammeter reading at that instant is the pixel's value. Raster the beam and the readings fill the 2D map.

Chapter 8 · how the part is wired during test

8. Test configurations — pin grounding

During a zap, only the pin under test is driven; the rest are either tied to ground or left floating. The default per ANSI/ESD STM5.5.1 and the JS-001 user guide is all other pins grounded — the worst case.[11][12]

Chapter 9 · keep them consistent

9. Zapping vs curve tracing — same grounding?

The standards mandate grounding for the zap; DC curve tracing has no mandated scheme. Best practice is to match them so the before/after leakage comparison is apples-to-apples — coupling and parasitics are configuration-dependent.[13]

Configuration aspect → TLP / ESD zapping vs DC I–V curve tracing
AspectTLP / ESD zappingDC I–V curve tracing
Pin under test→ TLP source (rectangular pulse, 0→V)→ SMU high (DC sweep 0→V_op)

Supply (VDDIO)GroundedGrounded (match)
Ground / PGNDReference (0 V)SMU low / reference (same node)
DrivePulse: ns–µs, high current (A)DC steady-state, low current (pA–µA)
SensingV & I probes on scope (transient)Kelvin / 4-wire (removes lead R)

Rule of thumb

Curve-trace with the identical grounding you zap in. Then a jump like 50 pA → 100 µA on the same pin is unambiguous damage, not a coupling artifact.
